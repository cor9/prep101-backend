Juniper's Audition Preparation Guide

1. PROJECT OVERVIEW
Project Type: Feature Film (Independent Drama)

Genre: Coming-of-age drama with spiritual undertones

Tone & Style: This is NOT a Disney Channel movie. This is quiet, observational, poetic cinema. Think soft focus on hard truths. The performances need to feel like documentary footage of real teenagers living real lives‚Äîno theatrical "acting," no melodrama, no performed emotion. Everything is earned. Everything is felt.

Comparable Projects: The Tree of Life (Terrence Malick), Moonlight, Eighth Grade, early episodes of Friday Night Lights, Wildlife with Carey Mulligan

Visual Language: Golden hour light. Long silences. Voiceover that acts as poetry, not exposition. The camera lingers. Your face will tell more story than your words ever could.

Scene Context: Juniper enters Tucker's world slowly, persistently, gently‚Äîlike water finding cracks in stone. These scenes span weeks, maybe months. We're watching the beginning of something neither of them fully understands yet. A boy grieving his father. A girl escaping hers. Two people learning what it means to carry something together.

Director's Vision Alert: This script was written with extreme intentionality. Every pause matters. Every repeated phrase ("TuckerTolly," the book, the tree) is a motif, not an accident. Your performance needs to honor that poetry without PLAYING poetry. Stay grounded. Stay human. Let the beauty happen around you, not through you performing it.
2. CHARACTER BREAKDOWN
Who is Juniper Jamieson? (The Real Version)
Juniper is a 14-year-old girl who has learned to make herself small so she doesn't take up space her father might need for his rage. She's learned that love and pain can live in the same hand. She's learned that mothers leave, fathers forget, and if you want anything resembling safety, you build it yourself.

But here's the miracle: she hasn't hardened. She hasn't turned mean or cynical or cold. Instead, she's become soft‚Äîdangerously soft‚Äîin a world that punishes softness. She still believes in signs. She still draws trees in books. She still shows up at Tucker's door every single morning, crooked grin steady, offering him a reason to keep walking forward.

Juniper is not a victim. She's a survivor who refuses to survive ugly.

How She Sees Herself:
As someone who doesn't quite belong anywhere. Not at home (where her dad is either absent or drunk). Not at school (we never see her there with other friends). She exists in the in-between‚Äîthe walk to and from school, the crack in Tucker's armor, the space between what is and what could be. She sees herself as the girl carrying the heavy book, hoping someone will offer to help.

How Tucker Sees Her:
As someone who "just steps into your life like they've got the spare key." She terrifies him because she sees him‚Äîreally sees him‚Äîand doesn't look away. She's patient where the world has been demanding. She's present where everyone else has left. She's the opposite of grief: she's hope with braided hair and orange painted sneakers.

How the WORLD Sees Her:
Probably doesn't. That's the tragedy and the power. She's invisible until she decides to be seen. And when she is? She's unforgettable.

Your Bridge to Juniper (Find Your Connection):
Have you ever loved someone who was too broken to love you back properly?
Have you ever had to parent yourself because the adults were too lost?
Do you know what it's like to make yourself small so you don't upset someone?
Have you ever stayed loyal to someone even when it hurt?
Do you believe in signs? In trees? In the idea that love can be quiet and still be real?
The Empathy Stretch (What's Different About Her):
You probably have more stability at home than Juniper does
You might not understand what it's like to have a parent who forgets you exist
You may not have had to develop her level of emotional self-sufficiency at 14
Her stillness isn't shyness‚Äîit's survival strategy. Learn the difference.
Character Reference Shortcut: Think early Carey Mulligan in An Education‚Äîa girl with an old soul who sees the world clearly but still chooses kindness. Or Kaitlyn Dever in Short Term 12‚Äîthat specific brand of wounded but not broken. Juniper is grace under pressure. She's a dogwood tree in a storm: she bends, but she doesn't snap.
3. UTA HAGEN'S 9 QUESTIONS (Juniper Edition)
Who am I?
I'm a girl who learned early that people leave. My mom left when I was eight‚Äîjust gone, no explanation good enough to fill that hole. My dad stayed, but only in body. His mind checks out every time he opens a bottle. I've been raising myself since third grade. I do my own laundry. I make my own meals. I forge his signature on permission slips. I know which nights to stay at a friend's house (when he works the late shift at the refinery and comes home already drunk). I know which bruises to cover and which lies to tell.

But I also know that trees grow even in drought. I know that some boys carry books without being asked. I know that Tucker Tolly kisses his mom goodbye and that makes him different. That makes him safe. And I've decided‚Äîquietly, carefully‚Äîthat I'm going to make him see me. Not because I need saving. Because I think maybe we can save each other.

Where am I?
Scene 1: Tucker's front door. This is sacred ground. This house has a mother who makes lunches "like armor." This house smells like fabric softener and routine. I want to live here. I want to belong here. So I keep showing up.

Scene 2: The high school parking lot. Exposed. Public. This is me making my move‚Äîcrooked grin, heavy book, zero shame. If I'm going to make Tucker notice me, it has to be out in the open where he can't hide.

Scene 3-5: The walk home. This is our world now. Cracked sidewalks, chain-link fences, the white church on the corner. These streets know my secrets. They've seen my dad's car weaving. They've seen me walk home alone a thousand times. But now I'm not alone. Now I have Tucker. And that changes everything.

What time is it?
Early fall. First weeks of high school. The time of day when the light turns everything gold and you can almost believe the world is kind. These are the afternoons that feel like they could last forever‚Äîbefore homework, before dinner, before my dad comes home and the air turns heavy. Right now, in this moment, I get to be a girl walking home with a boy who carries my book. Right now, I'm normal. I'm safe. I'm seen.

What surrounds me?
Stillwater, Oklahoma. A town small enough that everyone knows everyone's business, which means everyone knows my mom left and my dad drinks. The church bells ring every hour. The high school stadium lights flicker on at dusk for football practice. Pickup trucks and poverty and prayer‚Äîthat's Stillwater. It's not cruel, just indifferent. And I've learned to find beauty in the cracks: the way sprinklers catch light, the stained glass scattering colors on concrete, the sound of Tucker's voice when he almost smiles.

What are the relationships?
Tucker: He's my project and my person. I see his grief like I see my own loneliness‚Äîwe're both orphans in different ways. I'm teaching him it's okay to let someone in. He's teaching me it's okay to ask for help (even if I have to trick him with a heavy book).

Mom Tolly: She's everything I wish I had. Warm. Present. Protective. When she teases Tucker about "his friend," I want to correct her: I'm not just his friend. I'm going to be more. I'm going to be the person who stays.

My Dad: I love him and I hate him and I'm terrified of him and I'm worried about him. All at the same time. All the time. When he yells, I know it's the alcohol. When he forgets me, I know it's the grief. But knowing doesn't make it hurt less. I tell Tucker "it was just an accident" because I need to believe it. Because if it wasn't an accident, then what does that make me?

What are the circumstances?
I'm in survival mode pretending to be a normal teenager. My dad got arrested (DUI, not his first). I spent the night with a stranger from Social Services who smelled like menthol cigarettes and pity. I came to school the next day with a bruise on my cheek and a story about reaching for the radio. I've learned that if you smile when you lie, people believe you. I've learned that boys like Tucker will carry your books if you let them feel needed. I've learned that love doesn't look like the movies‚Äîit looks like showing up every single morning, even when you don't have to.

What do I want?
Immediate goal: Make Tucker see me. Not as the weird girl who won't go away. As someone worth choosing. Someone worth keeping.

Deeper want: To belong to something that doesn't leave. A person, a place, a promise that stays. I want what Tucker has‚Äîa mom who worries, a routine that holds, a home that feels like home. And if I can't have that for myself, maybe I can borrow his. Maybe if I make myself essential to his story, I get to be part of something good.

Deepest want (the one I barely admit to myself): I want to stop carrying everything alone. I want someone to see the bruises I hide and the fear I swallow and the exhaustion I wear like a second skin. I want Tucker to look at me and say, "I've got you." And mean it.

What's in my way?
Tucker's grief. He's so lost in missing his dad that he can't see I'm right here, ready to be found. His mom's protectiveness (she knows I'm trouble, even if she's too kind to say it). My dad's drinking (any day he could get worse, could hurt me for real, could make me disappear the way my mom did). The world's indifference (nobody's coming to save me, so I have to save myself). And my own fear that even if Tucker does choose me, even if he does see me‚Äîwhat happens when he realizes I'm not worth the trouble?

How do I get what I want?
Patience. Persistence. Presence. I show up every morning with my crooked grin and my heavy book. I make myself useful (he gets to feel like a gentleman). I make myself interesting (trees, drawings, metaphors that make him think). I don't rush him. I don't demand. I just... exist beside him until the space between us feels smaller. Until walking home without me feels wrong. Until the idea of me not being there is worse than the fear of letting me in.

And when he finally asks if I'm his girlfriend? I don't make it easy. I make him say it. I make him choose it. Because I need to know he means it.

4. SCENE-BY-SCENE BREAKDOWN
SCENE ONE: The Front Door
What I'm Physically Doing:

Standing in the doorway (threshold = symbolic, I'm asking permission to enter his world)
Balancing the book on my knees while I pet Meatball (grounding action, shows comfort with animals/innocence)
Lifting the book like an offering when I mention the Simpsons reference
Kneeling (making myself smaller, non-threatening)
Beaming when his mom suggests tomorrow (hope is my superpower)
Subtext Under the Lines:

"Is Tucker Tolly home?" = I know he's avoiding me. I'm giving him an out. But I'm not leaving.
"I brought a book this time." = I'm learning. I'm adapting. I'm making this easier for him.
"That's how Marge got Homer." = I'm telling you my strategy. I'm not hiding that I'm pursuing him. I'm bold enough to be honest about what I want.
"I can wait. I don't mind." = I have nowhere else to be. This is the best part of my day. You're the best part of my day.
Physicality Note: You're working with a DOG in this scene. Use Meatball. Let yourself get lost in petting him‚Äîit shows your capacity for tenderness, for connection, for finding joy in small moments. When you kneel, your body language should read as OPEN, not desperate. You're not begging. You're inviting. There's a difference.
SCENE TWO: "Is It Heavy?"
What I'm Physically Doing:

Walking across the parking lot (alone, carrying my big book, visible to everyone)
Stopping when he calls out (heart leap, don't show it too much)
Turning to face him (this is the moment I've been waiting for)
Playing dumb about the book's weight (testing him, flirting)
Catching on to his game and matching his energy (oh, we're doing THIS now? Okay.)
Realizing I'm about to win and leaning into the bit
Subtext:

"What?" = Make me spell it out. Make this mean something.
"Of course not... it's paper, not lead." = I'm not going to make this easy for you. You have to actually TRY.
"It's heavy! REALLY heavy!" = Okay, you tried. I'll meet you halfway. Let's do this dance.
The Comedy Here: This is a COURTSHIP RITUAL disguised as a conversation about a book. It's flirty without being cloying. It's playful without being childish. You're testing each other. He's offering to help. You're making him work for it. By the end, you're both smiling. That's the magic. Don't rush it. Let the pauses breathe. Let us see you falling for him in real time.
SCENE THREE: The First Walk
What I'm Physically Doing:

Walking steady at his side (matching his pace, not ahead, not behind‚Äîequal)
Glancing at him when he's quiet (studying him, learning him)
Noticing his shoes, his body language, the way he holds my book
Tilting my head when I speak (curiosity, openness, invitation)
Smiling small when he says something real (rewarding his honesty)
Looking back when I dare him to follow (playful confidence, I know he will)
Taking off ahead (testing him: will you chase me?)
Subtext:

"You always this quiet after school?" = I want to know you. All of you. Even the quiet parts.
"Maybe you just save it all up for later." = I see you. I see that you're more than this silence.
"Most boys would've just let me carry it myself." = You're different. That's why I chose you.
"That's my whole brain you're holdin' there." = I'm trusting you with something precious. Don't drop it.
"Maybe you were meant to carry it." = This isn't random. This is fate. We're supposed to find each other.
"Thanks for not makin' me carry it alone." = I've been alone for so long. Thank you for changing that.
Tucker's V.O. you need to FEEL:
"Sometimes I guess you don't know the weight you've been draggin'... 'til somebody lets you hold somethin' that ain't yours. And suddenly... yours just don't feel so heavy anymore."

That's what you're doing for him. You're giving him something to carry that's lighter than his grief. That's the gift. That's the love.
SCENE FOUR: The Kiss
What I'm Physically Doing:

Giving him a kiss on the front porch (brave, public, claiming him)
Waiting for him by the wrestling room (patience, loyalty)
Sitting cross-legged on the curb (grounded, at home in uncomfortable places)
Drawing the tree with my Sharpie (creating beauty, offering metaphor)
Showing him the drawing (vulnerability: this is how I see us)
Standing when he crouches (mirroring, connection)
Taking my book back (but letting him carry it again immediately‚Äîthe ritual continues)
Subtext:

"Did he though? Is that really who he let in. Your girlfriend?" = Say it. Out loud. Make it real.
[The kiss] = I choose you. I choose you. I choose you.
"You know what trees do? They hold on." = I'm telling you I'm not leaving. Even when it's hard. Even when you push me away. I'm a tree. I'm staying.
"It's where I'd go if the world felt too loud." = I'm giving you my safe place. I'm letting you in.
The Kiss: This is not a Disney Channel peck. This is a 14-year-old girl claiming her person. It's quick, repeated (peck, peck, peck), giggling, nervous, joyful, REAL. Don't perform it. LIVE it. If you've never had a first kiss, imagine the best moment of your life and put that feeling in your body. That's what this is for Juniper.
SCENE FIVE: The Confession
What I'm Physically Doing:

Walking beside him in silence (letting him lead the conversation)
Eyes flicking away when he mentions the police (shame, even though it's not my fault)
Tracing the chain-link fence (nervous gesture, grounding)
Shrugging off the weight of my words (performing casualness to survive the pain)
Adjusting my braid (self-soothing, buying time)
Looking at him when I say "girlfriend" (testing: does this scare you? Are you going to leave now?)
Subtext:

"It's really no big deal." = It's a huge deal. But if I say that, I'll break. So I'm lying to both of us.
"I just think he forgets sometimes." = I'm making excuses for him because I need to believe he's still in there somewhere.
"She left when I was eight." = I'm telling you the worst thing that ever happened to me. Please don't flinch.
"The bruise... it was just an accident." = I need you to believe this lie so I can keep believing it.
"He'd never hurt me on purpose." = If I admit he would, then what? Where do I go? Who protects me?
CRITICAL ACTING NOTE: When Juniper talks about her dad's abuse, she MINIMIZES. She explains. She justifies. This is textbook trauma response. Your job is NOT to play "sad abuse victim." Your job is to play a girl who has convinced herself she's fine. The tragedy isn't in what she says‚Äîit's in how hard she's working to make it sound normal. THAT'S what will break the audience's heart.

Juniper moves like water‚Äîfluid, adaptable, always finding the path of least resistance. She doesn't storm or stomp. She doesn't demand space. She EARNS it.

6. VOICE & SPEECH
Vocal Qualities:
Soft but clear: She doesn't shout. She doesn't need to. People lean in to hear her.
Oklahoma accent (subtle): Don't go full country. Think more "plains states" than "Southern drawl." Words like "gonna" and "ain't" and "sorta" are natural, not performed.
Pauses: Juniper thinks before she speaks. She's deliberate. She chooses her words.
Moments of lightness: When she's flirting or playful, her voice lifts. When she's confessing trauma, it flattens‚Äîself-protection.
Speech Patterns:
She's not verbose. She's efficient. She says what needs saying and nothing more.
She uses metaphor naturally (the tree, the book, "roots dig down deeper")
When she's lying about her dad, her sentences get longer‚Äîoverexplaining is a tell
Vocal Risk: What if during the "She left when I was eight" moment, Juniper's voice doesn't break‚Äîit goes FLAT. Emotionless. Like she's said it so many times it's lost meaning. That deadness might be more devastating than tears. Try both in rehearsal. See which one guts you more.

8. THE TREE METAPHOR (Your Thematic Anchor)

10. BOLD ACTING CHOICES THAT BOOK ROLES
The Danger Zone:
Playing Juniper as JUST sweet, JUST soft, JUST gentle = forgettable. Thousands of actresses can do "nice girl." You need to find the STEEL underneath the silk.

Surprising Shifts to Try:
Choice #1: The Laugh
What if when Tucker asks "Is it heavy?" and you say "Of course not... it's paper, not lead"‚Äîwhat if you LAUGH at him? Not mean. Just genuinely amused that he's being so transparent. It shows confidence. It shows you're not desperate. It shows you're PLAYING with him, not performing for him.

Choice #2: The Anger
When your dad pulls up drunk and yells at you‚Äîwhat if there's a FLASH of rage in your eyes before you put the mask back on? Just one second where we see: she's furious. She's humiliated. She hates him in this moment. Then she swallows it and says "It's just my dad" in that dead voice. The contrast will KILL.

Choice #3: The Certainty
When you say "Maybe you were meant to carry it" about the book‚Äîwhat if that line has WEIGHT? What if you believe in fate, in signs, in the universe putting people in each other's paths? Let that spiritual certainty come through. It adds dimension.

Choice #4: The Fear
After you kiss Tucker and claim him as your boyfriend‚Äîwhat if there's a moment where you FREEZE? Like, "Oh god, what did I just do? What if he leaves now that he knows how much I need him?" Let us see the vulnerability AFTER the brave choice. That's real.

The Audition Trap: Do NOT play the scenes in order emotionally. Scene 5 (the trauma confession) is not the "big emotional scene." Scene 4 (the kiss, the tree, the claiming of love) is just as high-stakes. If you only bring intensity to the "sad" scenes, you're telling casting you don't understand the character. Juniper's JOY is just as powerful as her pain. Maybe more so.

13. SAFETY & EMOTIONAL PREPARATION
This role requires you to access real pain‚Äîabandonment, abuse, fear, loneliness. Before you do that work, let's talk about how to do it SAFELY.

Before You Rehearse:
Set a timer. Give yourself permission to step OUT of Juniper's world when the work is done.
Have a "return to self" ritual: wash your face, change clothes, listen to happy music.
Talk to a parent/guardian about the material. Make sure you have support.
